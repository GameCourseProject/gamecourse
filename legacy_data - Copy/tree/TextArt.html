Text Art

Way back in the day, we didn't have fancy graphics cards, just text-based terminals. This didn't mean we couldn't have graphics. We just needed to do them using characters! Welcome to the fantastic world of...

Check out the Wikipedia page on ASCII Art. Lots of relevant information and resources there (also, as always, Google is your friend). This thing even predates computers. I've seen it done using a typewriter in the 70s'. It has been used for pictures but also for gaming, such as in the notorious roguelike games nethack and, more recently, Dwarf Fortress (some of the most complex and games with a deeper gameplay there are):

Nowadays, we can even go beyond this! We have UNICODE, not just ASCII, and with it a wealth of graphical characters and symbols we can use. So, your task here is to produce some Unicode-Art! What could you do? Some ideas:

  • A map, for an imaginary roguelike game, such as the ones above
  • A screen from an imaginary side-scrolling game (think classic Mario or Sonic games), but drawn in text

What you must not do is something like this:

That thing is a conversion of a photo to ASCII. It fails for two reasons: it doesn't use Unicode, and it was automatically generated by some program. You must do yours by hand, choosing your characters, etc.

So, the requirements for your work are:

  • Use the Everson Mono font, which is a monospaced Unicode font with lots of glyphs.
  • You should create your art as the only content in a stand-alone HTML file (with appropriate headers for UTF-8 and CSS for Everson Mono) or a Microsoft Word document.
  • Your art should prominently feature unicode characters
  • In your post, include the file with the art and a image of it, inline in the post, to make recognition easier (a screen capture is ok). Also, in the text of the post, discuss the main things you had to do, the most interesting characters you used, etc.
  • We may ask for improvements/changes before accepting the result, especially if the quality or originality requirements aren't met.
